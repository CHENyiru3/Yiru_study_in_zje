    Summary of Genetic Modification Techniques¶

    Traditional Methods¶

    • Homologous Recombination: Uses DNA constructs with homologous sequences to target specific genes in embryonic stem cells. Relatively inefficient but precise for gene knockouts and knockins.
    • Pronuclear Injection: Direct injection of DNA into fertilized eggs for random integration of transgenes. Useful for overexpression studies but lacks targeting precision.
    • Gene Trapping: Uses vector insertion to disrupt and report gene expression. Efficient for large-scale mutagenesis screening.

    Site-Specific Recombination Systems¶

    • Cre/loxP System: Uses Cre recombinase to excise DNA between loxP sites. Enables conditional knockouts when Cre expression is controlled by tissue-specific promoters.
    • Flp/FRT System: Similar to Cre/loxP but uses Flp recombinase and FRT recognition sites. Often used in combination with Cre/loxP for complex genetic manipulations.
    • Inducible Systems: Tet-On/Off and tamoxifen-inducible Cre (CreERT2) provide temporal control of gene expression or deletion.

    Modern Genome Editing¶

    • CRISPR/Cas9: Revolutionary technique using guide RNAs to direct Cas9 nuclease to specific DNA sequences. Creates precise double-strand breaks for gene knockout, knockin, or base editing. Highly efficient and versatile.
    • CRISPR Base Editors: Modified Cas9 systems fused to deaminases that convert specific nucleotides without double-strand breaks (C→T or A→G).
    • CRISPR Prime Editing: Combines Cas9 with reverse transcriptase to make precise edits without donor DNA templates.

    RNA Interference¶

    • shRNA/siRNA: Short hairpin or small interfering RNAs that target specific mRNAs for degradation, providing transient or stable knockdown rather than complete knockout.

    Transgenesis Enhancements¶

    • BAC Transgenesis: Uses bacterial artificial chromosomes to incorporate large DNA fragments with regulatory elements for more physiological expression patterns.
    • TARGATT/PhiC31: Site-specific integration systems that reduce position effects by targeting transgenes to predetermined genomic locations.

    Validation Methods¶

    • PCR/Sequencing: Confirms correct targeting and sequence integrity.
    • Southern Blotting: Verifies genomic integration and copy number.
    • Western Blotting: Confirms protein expression changes.
    • Functional Assays: Tests physiological consequences of genetic modifications.

    These techniques have revolutionized biomedical research by enabling precise manipulation of genomes to study gene function, model diseases, and develop therapeutic approaches.

    2. Model System¶

    • Choice of Mice:
    • Relevance: Mice (Mus musculus) are used due to their genetic similarity to humans (99% homologs), rapid reproduction, and inbred strains for reduced variability.

    • Ease of Manipulation: Sequenced and annotated genomes facilitate precise genetic modifications (e.g., via CRISPR/Cas9).

    • Alternative Models:

    • Pros: Rats (larger size), zebrafish (transparency), or cell cultures (lower cost).

    • Cons: Limited translatability to humans (e.g., zebrafish) or technical challenges (e.g., rat genome editing).

    • Cre/loxP for conditional knockouts and Tamoxifen-inducible Cre for temporal control.

    • Method Selection:

    • CRISPR is favored for speed and efficiency, though it risks off-target effects.

    • Cre/loxP enables cell-specific targeting but may have leaky expression.
